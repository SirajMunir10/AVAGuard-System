================================================================================
SECTION 17: COMPLIANCE CONTEXT FOR PENTEST FINDINGS
================================================================================

Penetration test findings do not exist in isolation and must be contextualized within the organization's regulatory and compliance obligations. Connecting technical vulnerabilities to specific compliance requirements increases their business relevance and helps organizations prioritize remediation based on regulatory exposure in addition to technical severity.

PCI DSS version 4.0 has specific requirements that directly correspond to common penetration test findings. Requirement 6.3 mandates that all system components are protected from known vulnerabilities by installing applicable security patches and updates, making unpatched systems with known CVEs a direct PCI DSS violation. Requirement 8.3 requires multi-factor authentication for all access into the cardholder data environment, making missing MFA on administrative interfaces a compliance gap. Requirement 11.4 explicitly requires penetration testing of all in-scope systems at least annually and after significant changes. Finding types related to unencrypted card data in transit map to Requirement 4.2, while access control findings map to Requirement 7.

HIPAA Security Rule requirements map to several common vulnerability categories. The Technical Safeguards at 164.312 require access controls at 164.312(a)(1), audit controls at 164.312(b), integrity controls at 164.312(c), and transmission security at 164.312(e). A penetration test finding of unencrypted transmission of electronic protected health information is a direct violation of 164.312(e)(1). Excessive user permissions that allow access to PHI beyond what is required for job function violate the minimum necessary access principle underlying the access control requirement.

SOC 2 Trust Services Criteria map penetration findings to the Common Criteria. CC6 covers logical and physical access controls, making access control vulnerabilities directly relevant to CC6.1 through CC6.8. CC7 covers system operations and monitoring, making findings related to unpatched vulnerabilities relevant to CC7.1 and logging failures relevant to CC7.2. CC8 covers change management and maps to findings related to insecure deployment practices and missing code review processes. Penetration test findings that are relevant to SOC 2 audits should be flagged as audit evidence and communicated to the compliance team.

ISO 27001:2022 Annex A controls provide a framework for mapping technical findings to organizational security requirements. A.8.8 covers management of technical vulnerabilities and applies directly to unpatched systems. A.5.15 and A.8.2 cover access control and apply to privilege escalation and excessive permission findings. A.8.24 covers use of cryptography and applies to weak cryptographic implementations. A.8.28 covers secure coding and applies to injection vulnerabilities and other application security issues. Including these control references in finding documentation helps organizations understand the remediation effort in the context of their existing compliance program.
